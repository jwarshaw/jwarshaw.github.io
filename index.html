Riding High

The Problem

Last year I bought a 2005 KHS Flite 300, an entry level road bike, on CL. I spent months searching. It was in excellent condition. It rode fast. The price was right. Perfect. Or so I thought. As I went on longer and more frequent rides I began to develop shoulder pain. I adjusted the seat. Still discomfort. The stem. No change. Added handlebar pads. Nothing. Before I bought the bike I researched sizes and determined, based on my height (5'10"), that a 56cm frame would be the right fit. I used a table like this. After further research, prompted by my riding pains, this method turned out to be a naive approach to sizing. I read up on bike fit and learned that it is a common problem among road cyclists. A better approach is to use a fit tool like this from Competitive Cyclist. I also found the source of my problem: my body proportions are not exactly average.

The Proportions

My limbs are long relative to my height. Or you might say my torso is short. I prefer the former. A typical bike frame is built such that the size indicates both the length of the seat tube and the length of the top tube (if you don't know your bike parts check out the diagram below). I am built such that this way of building bikes sucks for me. When I selected a 56cm bike I was half right. The bottom half. My legs which align with the seat tube fit a 56cm frame. My torso which aligns with the top tube fits a 53cm frame. My 56cm KHS is pulling my shoulders too far forward causing upper back pain. A tall and narrow bike is what I need. These are hard to find and custom build would break the bank. My options are limited, but I have a plan!



Source

The Plan

According to some randos on the internet, the top tube length is the main determinant for riding comfort. This (let's call it a)fact in combination with the idea that one can relatively easily "add" height and width to a frame via the seatpost and stem, form the basis of the plan. Here is the plan. I will purchase a 53cm bike on CL for cheapsies and install a long seat post and a long quill stem, effectively adding the height my legs require.
